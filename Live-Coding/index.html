Impact

Grace Hopper (1906–1992) was an American computer scientist and U.S. Navy rear admiral. She developed the first compiler for a computer programming language and contributed to the creation of COBOL, a widely used business programming language. Hopper was a key figure in making programming more accessible and coined the term "debugging." Known for her leadership and mentorship, she received numerous awards and remained an influential figure in tech throughout her life.

Early Life and Education

Grace Hopper was born on December 9, 1906, in New York City. She showed an early interest in mathematics and science, and as a child, she disassembled alarm clocks to understand how they worked. Hopper attended Vassar College, where she earned a degree in mathematics and physics in 1928. She went on to pursue graduate studies at Yale University, earning a master’s degree in mathematics in 1930 and a Ph.D. in 1934. Her academic achievements laid the foundation for her future groundbreaking work in computer science.

Career

Grace Hopper’s career began in academia before she joined the U.S. Navy during World War II, where she worked on the Mark I, one of the first electromechanical computers. After the war, she continued to contribute to computer science, developing the first compiler—a program that translates written code into machine-readable instructions.

This innovation led to the creation of high-level programming languages, including COBOL, which became widely used in business and government. Hopper also worked as a trailblazer for women in technology, mentoring others and advocating for accessible programming.

Throughout her career, she received numerous honors, including the Presidential Medal of Freedom, and remained an influential figure in tech until her death in 1992.

Retirement

After retiring from the U.S. Navy in 1966, Grace Hopper continued to make significant contributions to the field of computing. She joined Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC), where she worked as a senior consultant, promoting the use of computers in business and government. In 1967, she was recalled to active duty in the Navy, where she served as a rear admiral until her final retirement in 1986.

Even after retiring from the Navy, Hopper remained active in the tech community, giving lectures and advocating for computer science education, particularly for women in STEM. She continued to receive recognition for her pioneering work and remained a prominent figure in computing until her death in 1992.
